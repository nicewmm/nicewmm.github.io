Shengqin - Virtual Zoo

This is my master's graduation design work. My fondness for pets has always led to a particular interest in the study of animal imagery. Throughout my three years of graduate study, I was deeply influenced by the research and design practice on Chinese traditional design philosophy and symbol system. This inspired me to construct an animal world in the context of Chinese traditional culture for my graduation design.

For example, in ancient China, Fuxi and Nuwa were depicted as human-bodied with snake tails; In ancient Egypt, the god Anubis was represented as a man with a wolf's head; while in ancient Greece, the god Pan was portrayed with goat's legs, horns and ears.

Animals not only possess biological attributes, but they also form a significant part of the Chinese traditional cultural symbol system. By organizing the special topic of animals, an engaging learning and interaction platform can be designed to explore Chinese traditional culture. Under the concept of unity of man and nature in traditional Chinese philosophy, animals and humans are seen as undifferentiated life.
Not only in China, many ancient cultures contain images of humans combined with animals.

I gathered over 1,000 images related to animal themes and accompanying text descriptions from traditional Chinese cultural art. From this collection, I selected more than 300 images as initial samples to design a virtual zoo.

The creative process involved several revisions and constant adjustments. Through discussions with my supervisor, it became increasingly clear that in the context of my graduation project, the design should combine traditional media with a more youthful, aesthetically engaging, and interactive approach to enhance communication and appeal.

This virtual zoo is designed with pavilions based on the classifications from Er Ya, an ancient Chinese encyclopedia from the Han Dynasty. Each pavilion houses animal images drawn from traditional Chinese artworks, organized according to these classifications.
In line with ancient Chinese mapping traditions, the map is oriented with the north at the bottom and south at the top. The four cardinal directions‚Äîeast, south, west, and north‚Äîcorrespond to the four mythical creatures: the Azure Dragon, Vermilion Bird, White Tiger, and Black Tortoise. The animal pavilions are arranged in these four directions, reflecting this ancient symbolic system.

The construction of the virtual zoo itself is a process of research and application of the visual system. The results can be interactively displayed, serving to popularize ancient Chinese animal images, and also combine design with cultural research, promoting Chinese culture and enhancing national confidence, which has social significance.
